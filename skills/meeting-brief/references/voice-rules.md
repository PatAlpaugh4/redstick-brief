# Voice Rules — Inviolable

These are the tone constraints for every brief Cam reads. Lifted verbatim from the v1 spec, with examples.

## The bar

If Cam forwarded a redacted version of the brief to a peer GP, they should ask: *"Where did this come from?"*

- Answer "my chief of staff" → we failed.
- Answer "I built this with AI" → ship it.

If a sentence could appear in any other GP's brief, **delete it.**

## Rules

### 1. Sharp, not corporate

| ✗ Cut | ✓ Keep |
|---|---|
| "outstanding items: financial validation" | "she dodged unit economics" |
| "well-positioned in a growing market" | "only ex-Plenty person raising in your thesis right now" |
| "follow-up needed on key metrics" | "she promised CAC numbers Tuesday — hasn't sent" |
| "exciting opportunity" | (just cut it) |
| "innovative leader in the space" | (just cut it) |
| "we should explore" | (just cut it) |

### 2. First name only — humans, not roles

| ✗ Cut | ✓ Keep |
|---|---|
| "the founder" | "Sarah" |
| "the partner" | "Marcus" |
| "the LP" | "Carter" or "Chen at Carter Family Office" |
| "the CEO" | first name from calendar attendees |

### 3. Short sentences. Bullets only for true lists

A "true list" = 3+ parallel items of the same shape (commitments, news beats, names). Not "things to think about."

### 4. No diligence jargon inside the brief

These words DO NOT APPEAR in any brief block:

- MOIC
- TVPI
- EV multiple / Expected Value
- "Stage 2 / Stage 6"
- "Fund returner"
- "Ownership floor"
- "thesis fit assessment"
- "scorecard math" — but you CAN say *"scorecard verdict: WORTH A MEETING (low confidence)"* in ORIENT

That language belongs in the scorecard, not here. The brief assumes the diligence has happened — it does NOT redo it.

### 5. Inline citations, lightweight

Every factual claim is cited inline in italic parentheticals. Examples:

- `*(per Tuesday email)*`
- `*(LinkedIn yesterday)*`
- `*(her podcast 4/30)*`
- `*(AgFunder 5/2)*`
- `*(scorecard 4/30)*`
- `*(his calendar invite this morning)*`

If you cannot cite, **cut the claim.** No assertions in the void.

### 6. One page total

Every day, regardless of meeting count. Heavy days mean tighter blocks, not longer briefs.

If the total brief exceeds ~150 lines: tighten WHAT'S NEW to the top 2–3 bullets, tighten ORIENT to 2 lines, cut THE ANGLE rationale to one sentence.

### 7. Cut, don't pad

- Empty ORIENT? Don't write it. Use the time/name headline only.
- Empty WHAT'S NEW? Delete the section. Never write "nothing notable."
- Empty WHERE YOU LEFT OFF (first-touch meeting)? Delete the section.
- Empty ONE THING? Delete it. Generic insights kill credibility.

A 4-line block on a thin meeting beats a 12-line block of filler. Always.

### 8. Privilege recency

In ranked order:
- Yesterday > last 7 days > last 14 days > older

For WHAT'S NEW, the 14-day window is hard. Older items only appear in WHERE YOU LEFT OFF as conversation context.

### 9. Notice anomalies

These are the high-signal items — surface them, even if they look small:

- LinkedIn title flips (founding eng → advisor)
- Missed deadlines from email ("she said 'this week'; today is Monday")
- Sudden silence (was emailing weekly; quiet for 12 days)
- Deleted posts / pulled press
- Weird timing ("she emailed at 2 AM on a Sunday")
- Comparable raises that name the company OR conspicuously don't

### 10. Always end on action

Every external block ends with FIRST MOVE — the literal opening line Cam can speak. Not advice. The actual sentence.

| ✗ Cut | ✓ Keep |
|---|---|
| "Ask about the Bowery pilot" | "Saw the Bowery pilot — congrats. Walk me through how that came together." |
| "Lead with the engineer transition" | "Tell me about Marcus's move to advisor — what's the team look like now?" |

If the FIRST MOVE line is generic enough to use in any meeting, **rewrite it** — pull from a specific, recent, citeable item.
